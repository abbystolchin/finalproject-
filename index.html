What's Actually in Onondaga County's Water?

35 violations were recorded across local water systems in 2024. Some are just paperwork. Others aren't.

Most people don't think twice about turning on the tap. But in 2024, water systems across Onondaga County — from mobile home parks to village utilities to local businesses — racked up 35 violations of state and federal drinking water standards. Some of them are pretty mundane. Others are genuinely concerning.

The data comes from the New York State Department of Health, which tracks every time a water system fails to meet safety requirements, whether that's missing a monitoring deadline, filing paperwork late, or actually having something dangerous in the water. The state's Bureau of Water Supply Protection works with local health departments and water suppliers to make sure public water systems are regularly monitored and that operators are properly trained and certified.

How It Works

Every public water system in New York State receives a list of required monitoring tests each year. At the end of each monitoring period, the local health department reviews whether those tests were completed — and issues a violation notice to any system that missed one.

Missing a test doesn't automatically mean the water was unsafe. But water systems are legally required to collect samples to confirm their water meets state and federal standards. Skipping the test means there's no proof either way.

⚠ Key Finding

Rolling Hills Community LLC, a mobile home park in Onondaga County, tested positive for PFOS — a "forever chemical" linked to cancer, thyroid disease, and immune system damage. Residents were drinking water above the legal limit before corrective action was taken.

The one that stands out most is Rolling Hills Community LLC, a mobile home park that tested positive for PFOS — one of the so-called "forever chemicals" that have been turning up in water supplies across New York for years. These chemicals don't break down in the environment or in your body, and they've been linked to cancer, thyroid disease, and a weakened immune system. Rolling Hills logged the violation in early 2024, and the state says corrective action was taken — but residents were drinking water above the legal limit before that happened.

When a water system exceeds the maximum contaminant level, public notification is required within 30 days, and the system must certify to the local health department that residents were notified. That notification can come through publicly available media, posted online, or mailed directly to customers — depending on the severity of the violation.

Syracuse City's water system also appeared in the data, flagged for not properly monitoring for 1,4-dioxane, a likely carcinogen that's been found across New York State. That violation has since been resolved. In response to questions about the violation, the New York State Department of Health clarified that the City of Syracuse's 2025 Annual Water Quality Report showed 1,4-dioxane was tested and not detected, and that the 2023 report recorded results of 0.0025 µg/L — well below the maximum contaminant level of 1 µg/L.

Failure to collect a sample doesn't necessarily mean the drinking water safety was at risk. However, public water systems are required to collect the samples to ensure water meets state and federal drinking water standards.

Resolution status

Were the Violations Fixed?

Resolution status of violations

Here's the silver lining: every violation in the county was eventually resolved or had corrective action taken by the end of the year. Nothing is listed as still open. But "corrective action taken" has a specific meaning — according to the state DOH, it generally means the water supplier did something to address the violation, like collecting the missed samples, installing treatment, or taking a contaminated well offline. For specifics on what each system did, the DOH directed questions to either the public water system directly or the Onondaga County Health Department.

For residents at Rolling Hills, though, the damage — if any — was already done before action was taken.

Who's affected

Community Systems vs. Transient Systems

Community vs. non-community water systems

The majority of violations — 20 out of 35 — came from community water systems, meaning the kind that serve actual residents year-round, not just people passing through. That's the category that matters most for public health, and it's where the PFOS and disinfection byproduct issues showed up.

A big chunk of the remaining violations were operations report failures — basically water systems that didn't file required paperwork on time. Places like Vesper Hills Golf Club, Wildwood Event Center, and a handful of local restaurants and stores fell into this category. It's not great, but it's a very different problem than having toxins in the water.

On Mobile Home Parks

Mobile home parks with public water supplies fall under the jurisdiction of the local health department — or the State District Office if there's no local health department. The DOH notes that in New York State, the typical regulatory and enforcement process works at the majority of mobile home parks, though there are occasions where the state partners with other agencies or the Attorney General to coordinate enforcement.

The bigger picture here isn't that Onondaga County's water is in crisis — it's not. But the data is a reminder that the systems delivering water to your home aren't perfect, and that smaller, private water systems like mobile home parks often fly under the radar until something goes wrong.

All public water systems are also required to report their annual testing data to customers through Annual Water Quality Reports, published by the New York State Department of Health. If you want to check what's in your water, those reports are publicly available at health.ny.gov.

What happens when something goes wrong

Understanding Public Notification

Not all violations trigger the same response. According to the DOH, the type of notification required depends on the classification of the standard that was violated:

24-Hour Notice

For the most serious violations — usually an immediate health risk. Typically accomplished through publicly available media and website posting.

30-Day Notice

Required when a water system exceeds the maximum contaminant level (MCL). Notices must be mailed directly to all customers. The system must certify to the local health department that notification was completed.

12-Month Notice

For less urgent violations. Can be included in the system's Annual Water Quality Report, which all public water systems are required to send to customers every year.

Annual Water Quality Reports

Also called Consumer Confidence Reports, all public water systems must publish these annually. They include testing results, detected contaminants, and any violations from that year.
